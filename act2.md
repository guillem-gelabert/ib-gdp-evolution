Act II — Who Else Got Richer
Step 8 — The question
(Chart: Step 7's line holds — IB absolute GDP per capita, 1900–2024, climbing steeply after 1960.)

So: the line climbed. The islanders stopped being poor. And the story we tell ourselves is that tourism is what did it.
There is one way to check whether that's true. Look at who else got richer in the same years.

Step 9 — Extremadura enters
(Chart: Y-axis relabels to "GDP per capita, real € (2025)" — same axis, same scale. A second line fades in: Extremadura, 1900–2024. It starts lower than IB, stays flat through the first half of the century, then — around 1960 — it also bends and climbs.)

This is Extremadura. It is the poorest region in Spain. It has no coastline. It has no airport worth the name. In 1960, when the first charter flights were landing in Palma, Extremadura had roughly a million people, most of them working land that had been worked the same way for centuries.
Watch what its line does.

Step 10 — The shape is the same
(Chart: Extremadura's line completes. It doesn't match IB's level — it stays below — but the shape from 1960 onward is unmistakably similar. Both climb steeply. Both bend in the same decade.)

Extremadura's economy grew in the same decades as ours. Not as fast. Not as high. But the curve is the same curve: a long flat century, then a bend, then a climb.
Extremadura had no Magaluf. No Playa de Palma. No hotels built on the coast because there is no coast. And still, its line goes up. In the same years. For roughly the same reasons we are about to discuss.

Step 11 — And not just Extremadura
(Chart: More lines fade in. Galicia. Castilla-La Mancha. Portugal. Ireland. Each one starts flat, each one bends upward around the same decade. The chart begins to look like a forest of similar-shaped curves — different heights, same rhythm.)

It isn't just Extremadura. Galicia did it. Castilla-La Mancha did it. Portugal did it. Ireland did it faster than anyone.
What all of these places share is not tourism. What they share is the second half of the twentieth century: the end of autarky, the opening of trade, the arrival of foreign capital, the construction of welfare states, the long European peace, eventually EU membership. The tailwind was continental. Every region caught some of it.
We caught ours through tourism. Extremadura caught theirs through something else. Ireland caught theirs through yet another thing. The wind was the same wind.

Step 12 — The pivot
(Chart: All peer lines fade back to gray. Only IB remains in color. A beat.)

Which leaves us with an uncomfortable question. If the climb wasn't tourism — if the climb was a thing that was happening anyway, to regions that had nothing like our industry — then what, exactly, did tourism do for the Balearics?
There is one way to answer that. Stop asking how much. Start asking compared to whom.

Step 13 — The axis switch
(Chart: The whole chart re-animates. Y-axis transitions from "real €" to "% of EU-15 average (EU-15 = 100)". A horizontal reference line snaps in at 100. Every country's line re-scales. This is the single most important chart transition in the piece — it should be a held moment.)

Same data. Different question. Now the chart shows how each region compares, year by year, to the European average. The flat line at 100 is Europe itself. Above it: regions pulling ahead. Below it: regions falling behind.

Step 14 — The climb, revisited
(Chart: Highlight the IB line. Peer lines fade to gray. IB's line climbs through the 1960s, 70s, 80s — crosses 100 somewhere in the 1970s — keeps rising — peaks visibly around 1990, somewhere above the reference line.)

For three decades, the Balearics did something most European regions don't do: they didn't just grow, they caught up. The line crossed the European average in the 1970s. It kept climbing. By around 1990, the islands were meaningfully richer than the European average — one of the wealthier corners of the continent.
This is the part of the tourism story that looks strongest. For one long moment, the islands really were pulling ahead.

Step 15 — The drift
(Chart: IB line continues past 1990. It stops climbing. It plateaus. Then, gradually, it begins to slope downward. 2000, 2010, 2020. A shaded band marks "peak." The 2024 endpoint is visibly lower than the 1990 peak.)

And then it stopped.
Somewhere around 1990, the Balearic economy reached its highest point, relative to Europe, that it has ever reached — and began, quietly, to drift the other way. Not a crash. Not a crisis. A slow, uninterrupted slide that has now lasted longer than the climb that preceded it.
The islands today sit lower on the European scoreboard than they did when the cold war ended.

Step 16 — Peers continue
(Chart: Selected peer lines return in color. Ireland climbs dramatically past 1990. Malta climbs steadily. Portugal holds roughly level. IB stands out — the one line that rose, then fell.)

Meanwhile, the neighbors kept moving. Ireland kept climbing. Malta kept climbing. The Algarve held its ground. Extremadura kept converging — slowly, steadily, the way it had been doing the whole time.
The Balearics are among the few European regions that reached a peak, in relative terms, and then spent the following decades falling away from it.

Step 17 — The hinge
(Chart: Strip back to a single image — IB's relative-GDP line, the 1990 peak visible, the descent visible. Clean. No noise.)

This is the shape of the thing we don't usually talk about. The islands got richer — yes, really, a real, measurable climb — and then, for thirty-five years, fell slowly behind the continent they belong to.
The climb was not unique to us. The fall is.

Craft notes
The causal posture holds. At no point does the piece claim tourism caused the climb, or caused the fall. Step 11 explicitly names the continental tailwind — trade, welfare state, EU, peace, capital — and notes that tourism was one of several local forms that tailwind took. Step 17 lands the empirically defensible claim: the climb was common, the fall is ours.
Extremadura is doing almost all the work. The whole act rests on that single comparison. If your data is clean for Extremadura, the argument is nearly unassailable — it's the cleanest possible case (no tourism, same Spanish macro environment, same growth curve). If Extremadura's data is patchy pre-1960, substitute Castilla-La Mancha or Galicia, but keep the archetype: a Spanish region with no tourism industry that grew in the same window. The name doesn't have to be Extremadura, the function does.
The 1990 peak is drawn as a range, not a year. I deliberately wrote "around 1990," "somewhere around 1990," "in the 1990s." Your data may show a cleaner year; if it does, we can tighten. If it shows a range (say 1988–1993), the current language is already correct.
Ireland is optional but rhetorically useful. Including it in Step 16 does two things: it shows that post-1990 climbing was possible (so the Balearic flatness isn't a continental ceiling), and it neutralizes the likely counter-argument that "everyone plateaued after the 90s." If Irish data is awkward or distracts, it can come out — Malta alone does enough.
One intentional omission: no why. Act II ends on the observation, not the explanation. Why did the islands stop converging? Is it tourism-specific (productivity ceilings in hospitality, monoculture, low-wage lock-in)? Is it Spain-specific (national productivity stagnation)? Is it structural (islands, small markets)? The piece as currently drafted doesn't say — and I think that's right for Act II. Speculating on why belongs either in a short Act III or as a closing reflection. The reveal is stronger when the piece earns the right to the question before attempting an answer.
